What other simulators do well — and how we steal it

A competitive teardown of making/precision sims (Cooking Sim, PC Building Sim, Half-Life: Alyx, pottery, blacksmith, Skyrim lockpicking) read through one lens: how should our first-person woodturning sim handle camera, controls, hands, feedback, teaching, and immersion.

The wedge. Every shipping "woodturning" game today is a shallow outline-tracer — tap/drag to match a silhouette, no tool physics, no grip, no catch. Our differentiator is the combination none of them attempt: honest tool-to-wood physics + an audio-first correct/incorrect gradient + fading assistance + diegetic, machine-faithful controls. That's the design to protect.

The through-lineSix lessons that cut across every sim

If you read nothing else, these are the cross-cutting patterns the best making-sims share.

A precision toggle, not raw sensitivity

Cooking Sim (hold SHIFT for fine handling) and Skyrim lockpicking both solve our exact problem: a mouse is too coarse for delicate work. The win is a modal precision state with a clear feedback gradient as you near the sweet spot.

Hard, but learnable in minutes

Surgeon Simulator's designer: awkward works because you "adapt quite quickly" and failure is "funny, not embarrassing." Be honest and punishing (catches, tear-out) — but the loop to competence must be minutes, not hours.

Menus are physical things

Job Simulator's rule: progression can't be floating panels — it's cartridges you slot and levers you pull. Our lesson select, speed dial, and tool rack should live in the shop.

Show, then do — with invisible help

Half-Life: Alyx auto-turns knobs you reach for and lands "close enough" pulls. The player feels precise; the game quietly closes the gap. A coach can snap tool-rest contact and forgive the first approach.

Juice per contact, 500 times

Car Mechanic Sim is literally unscrewing bolts — it survives on per-bolt tactility. Our equivalent is per-pass chisel-to-wood: chips, pitch, surface change. It must feel good the 500th time.

The category is wide open

Existing woodturning games are outline-matchers with no tool physics. Our honest physics is the moat — the danger is accidentally regressing to silhouette-tracing.

Dimension 1Camera & point of view

PC Building Simulator

Locked at the bench

First-person fixed at a workbench that is the entire stage — you lean/orbit into the case, you don't roam mid-build.

Cooking Simulator

Roam, then station

Free-roam kitchen, but work happens at fixed stations — transition is pure proximity + holding a tool.

Flight / driving sims

Cockpit = the screen

Realistic modes enforce cockpit view and remove non-diegetic gauges. Precision work → lock the camera so the instruments are the frame.

Avoid: Surgeon Simulator's free-floating camera over a physics mess (great comedy, terrible for honest precision); and third-person/over-the-shoulder for the cut — close work wants eyes on the contact line.

Our lathe Three framed states: (a) workshop walk ~70–75° FOV for spatial comfort; (b) at-lathe locks to a fixed standing pose on the spindle axis, slight downward tilt onto the tool rest; (c) turning narrows FOV (~60°) and pushes in so the chip line and bevel dominate. Drive walk→lathe by proximity (we already have auto-lock), never by a cut. We've already killed the old teleport-to-void — this validates that instinct.

Dimension 2Controls

Cooking Simulator

Hold SHIFT = precision mode

Mouse = translate, LMB+vertical = raise/lower, RMB = rotate. Cutting is contextual: hold knife near meat, click the cut zone. The closest real analog to our mouse-driven tool.

Car Mechanic Sim

Hold + sweep to act

Continuous unscrew = hold LMB and sweep the cursor; stuck bolts glow and gate on a consumable. Continuous action = hold + mouse delta, with visual gating for special cases.

My Summer Car

Tools by fidelity

Ratchet for assembly, torque wrench for final spec — wrong torque fails later. Different tools carry different precision. (exact torque numbers are community-sourced)

Cockpits / Job Sim

Click-drag the knob

Dials and switches are grabbed and turned in-world, never adjusted by a 2D slider widget.

Avoid: Surgeon Sim's per-finger keys (intentionally clumsy — the opposite of teaching a craft); and turning the EVS dial into a HUD slider.

Our lathe Tool-in-hand: mouse XY = tool tip in the cutting plane (traverse + depth); the cut is implicit while the tip rides the wood — no click, like a real bevel rub. Reserve a SHIFT precision modifier (scale mouse delta ~4× down) for finishing/detail passes.
Two-handed grip: model it as one cursor, two constraints — mouse position is the front hand (tip); RMB/scroll is the back hand (bevel angle / tool rotation). A learnable two-axis skill, no second pointer.
EVS power/speed: a click-and-drag rotary knob in 3D with detents + a motor pitch that tracks RPM; power is a physical paddle you click.

Dimension 3Hand presence

Half-Life: Alyx

IK ↔ animation slider

Valve blends finger-tracking and canned animation per interaction so hands "feel natural — players don't think about them." The satisfying click of loading ammo is what sells tool presence.

Job Simulator

A small grip vocabulary

Owlchemy studied ~30 grips, shipped the 10–15 common ones. Refine a small set of correct poses; don't sprawl into full IK.

Avoid: uncanny half-finished IK — "just close enough to immersion to be distracting when it's not." Half-done hands are worse than none.

Our lathe We're mouse, not VR — so use canned, correct two-hand poses per tool (a roughing gouge held differently from a skew), rigidly attached to the tool mesh, tool driven by the cursor. The posture itself is coaching — back hand on the handle, front hand pinning the tool to the rest. Animate a small flex/recoil on a catch. Don't chase per-finger IK to the spinning wood. We just shipped a first-pass two-hand grip — this says: keep it canned + correct, add the catch-recoil.

Dimension 4Tool ↔ material feedback ("juice")

Skyrim lockpicking

Audio is the channel

Higher-pitched click near the sweet spot, harsh grind when wrong, pick snaps on force in the wrong place. Correct vs. wrong taught entirely by sound + resistance.

House Flipper

Surface-state reward

Dirt wipes away, paint fills coverage — the visible transformation is the payoff. Progressive surface state is the reward loop.

Cooking Simulator

Graded forgiveness

Yellow highlight = interactable; "Steady Hands" perk reduces rolling. Forgiveness is graded (weight matters more than exact shape), not binary.

Blacksmith sims

Each step its own feedback

Heat → hammer → temper → grind — every stage has its own feedback beat, so a long craft feels like real process.

Avoid: binary right/wrong (kills the skill curve); and the existing woodturning games' outline-match-only feedback — it teaches geometry, not technique.

Our lathe — the differentiator Bevel-rub vs. scrape vs. catch as an audio-first gradient: clean shear = steady hiss; too steep / dig-in = rising harsh tone → catch (tool kick + chip spray + thunk). Skyrim's sweet-spot/grind, applied to a gouge.
Chips + surface state: pre-pooled chip particles whose size/spray encode aggressiveness; the wood transitions torn → cut → finished sheen (House Flipper's reveal). Honor the no-heap-allocation tick — pre-allocate the buffers.
Forgiveness dial: early lessons widen catch tolerance and snap the bevel; advanced lessons remove the training wheels.

Dimension 5Teaching & tutorialization

PC Building Sim

Separate tutorial mode

A dedicated "How to Build" mode apart from career; inventory categorized so the next correct part is findable. Step-gated, low-stakes.

Woodturning 3D

Ghost outline (shallow)

A black silhouette to match — a good spatial target, but teaches only shape, never tool handling. Our opening.

Surgeon Sim

Failure as teacher

Failure is funny and low-shame, so players keep retrying. The retry loop is the real tutorial.

Our lathe We already have gated lessons + a coaching overlay. Push three ideas: Ghost tool, not just ghost shape — show a translucent ideal bevel-rub path the player chases in real time (fixes every competitor's blind spot: it teaches the motion). Step-gate the craft chain — mount → true the cylinder → rough to profile → finish → sand, each an independently green-able lesson (fits our slice model). Catch = teachable moment — don't end the lesson; pause, replay the last second highlighting the bad angle, resume. Low-shame retry.

Dimension 6Immersion & diegetic UI

Virtual cockpits

Instruments are the UI

Realism modes remove non-diegetic gauges entirely — the most immersive interface short of real hardware.

Job Simulator

UI is physical objects

Progression = cartridges + a lever, not a menu.

Avoid: going 100% diegetic and starving the player of the one or two numbers they truly need (current RPM, target diameter); and a persistent cluttered HUD that breaks the "real shop" feeling. The consensus: blend diegetic + minimal spatial HUD.

Our lathe The real Jet JWL-1642EVS has a digital RPM readout on the headstock — use that as the speed display (diegetic, and faithful to the machine whose dims live in content/lathe). Put the lesson objective on a paper spec card clipped to the bench, not a floating panel. Keep only a minimal coaching overlay that fades in near the contact point and out when idle — the cockpit-sim blend, not a full HUD. We already render the diegetic RPM readout + live digits — lean into it.

Dimension 7Onboarding the signature interaction

Half-Life: Alyx

Invisible assistance

The gravity-glove signature is eased in with auto-land help, full one-handed play, and left-handed options. Accessibility is the onboarding.

Surgeon Sim

Cheap, funny failure

Onboards an odd scheme by making early failure low-cost, so players keep experimenting until it clicks.

Let's Create! Pottery

The material teaches tempo

Slow drag = good, fast = ruined — taught purely through immediate material response, zero text.

Our lathe Mouse-tool onboarding: a zero-stakes "scratch pad" blank, motor off, no objective — just feel cursor → tip. Then motor on, generous catch tolerance, ghost path on; graduate the tolerance down over lessons (Alyx's fading assistance).
Webcam-pencil (stretch input): treat like Pottery's tempo lesson — first session is "draw a slow line in the air, watch the tool traverse"; success = "it moved smoothly," not "you matched a profile." Mouse stays an always-available fallback (Alyx's never-gate-content-behind-the-exotic-input rule).

Closest cousinsBest-in-class teardowns — what to steal

Cooking Simulator

The single most directly transferable game we found. SHIFT-to-precision turns a coarse mouse into fine manipulation — lift it wholesale for finishing cuts. Contextual contact (hold knife near meat → cut) maps to hold-gouge-near-spinning-wood → cut, no extra verb. Graded forgiveness (weight matters, not exact form) → grade our profile tolerance per lesson.

↳ Steal: SHIFT precision · contextual cut · yellow-highlight grabbables · graded tolerance

PC Building Simulator

Our assembly-lesson twin. Locked-at-bench camera + categorized inventory = the player always knows where to stand and what's next. A dedicated "How to Build" mode separate from career — and we are literally building the JWL-1642EVS, so that's our PCBS moment. The satisfaction of a part snapping into its slot with a confirming sound applies straight to our tailstock / banjo / tool-rest assembly.

↳ Steal: station-locked POV · a real assembly tutorial mode · snap-to-slot + confirm sound

Let's Create! Pottery

Proof that the material can teach tempo with zero UI (fast = ruined), and that a calm, low-failure creative loop sells to a broad audience — a counterweight to Surgeon-Sim chaos. Position woodturning as meditative craft mastery. Caution: pottery sims are outcome-focused (shape-matching) — don't inherit their lack of tool fidelity; that's exactly where we win.

↳ Steal: material-teaches-tempo · meditative framing · (avoid) outcome-only shaping

Do nextIdeas worth prototyping

Punchy, specific, and ranked roughly by bang-for-buck given what we've already built.

  1. Audio-first cut gradient — steady hiss = clean shear; rising harsh tone = nearing a catch; thunk + chip-spray = catch. (Skyrim lockpicking, applied to wood.) Our SFX foundation is already in.
  2. Ghost tool-path, not just ghost profile — chase a translucent ideal bevel line in real time. Teaches motion; beats every existing woodturning game.
  3. SHIFT precision mode — mouse delta scales ~4× down for finishing/detail cuts. (Cooking Sim.)
  4. One cursor, two constraints grip — mouse = front hand (tip), RMB/scroll = back hand (bevel angle). Learnable two-axis skill, no second pointer.
  5. Catch = teachable moment — pause, replay the last second highlighting the bad angle, resume. (Surgeon Sim's low-shame failure, made instructive.)
  6. Surface-state transformation — wood visibly goes torn → cut → polished sheen as you finish + sand. (House Flipper's reveal.)
  7. Fading assistance curve — early lessons snap the bevel + widen catch tolerance; advanced lessons strip it, shown as a progress signal. (Alyx auto-land.)
  8. Zero-stakes scratch-pad blank for onboarding both mouse and webcam-pencil before any graded lesson.
  9. Snap-to-slot assembly + confirm sound for the JWL-1642EVS parts. (PCBS install satisfaction.)
  10. Step-gated craft chain — mounted → trued → roughed → finished → sanded, each an independently green-able lesson slice.
  11. Click-drag rotary EVS knob with motor-pitch feedback + detents, diegetic RPM on the real headstock display. (We've built the readout + dial — add the pitch + detents.)
